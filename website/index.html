Overview

The Black-Scholes framework — and much of classical financial econometrics — assumes that daily stock returns are normally distributed. This project tests that assumption systematically against a hierarchy of four models fitted to 25 years of S&P 500 daily log-returns (2000–2024), spanning three major market crises.

The central question: by how much does the Gaussian model underestimate tail risk, and do Lévy-based distributions (Variance-Gamma and Normal Inverse Gaussian) offer a statistically meaningful improvement? Risk measures are evaluated both in-sample (MLE, AIC/BIC) and out-of-sample (rolling VaR backtests, Bayesian PSIS-LOO).

Model Hierarchy

Gaussian

2 parameters. Black-Scholes baseline. Symmetric, thin tails.

Student-t

3 parameters. Symmetric fat tails via degrees-of-freedom ν.

Variance-Gamma

4 parameters. Pure jump Lévy process. Finite variation, no Brownian component.

Normal Inv. Gaussian

4 parameters. Lévy process via IG subordination. Semi-heavy tails.

Data & Returns

6,287 daily log-returns on the S&P 500 index (^GSPC), computed as rt = log(St / St−1), covering 2 January 2000 to 31 December 2024. Four market shock periods are tracked throughout the analysis.

Roadmap

Weeks 3–4 introduce the Lévy models (VG, NIG) and conduct a full sub-period distributional analysis across the four shock periods. Week 5 extends estimation to Bayesian MCMC via PyMC (NUTS sampler), yielding posterior distributions over all parameters and formal predictive accuracy comparison via PSIS-LOO. Week 6 runs rolling VaR backtests and fits a regression of VG/NIG parameters on the VIX index as a predictor of distributional regime. Week 7 produces the final write-up.
