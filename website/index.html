The density that wins

The Normal Inverse Gaussian keeps its full four-parameter density, written in Week 4 as a custom PyTensor log-density through the modified Bessel function K₁, so NUTS gradients flow through it exactly.

The tail that matters

The Student-t's fitted degrees of freedom sit at ν̂ = 2.648, heavy enough that kurtosis is infinite. A power-law tail survives calm training data; an exponential one does not.

01
Foundations

Why leave Black-Scholes at all

A literature review and research proposal. Cont (2001) catalogues the stylised facts Gaussian returns can't produce: heavy tails, skew, volatility clustering. Madan, Carr & Chang (1998) supply the Variance-Gamma alternative; Christoffersen (1998) supplies the test that will judge everything at the end.

02
First fits

The tail is heavier than you think

Gaussian and Student-t fitted by MLE. The t wins by 1,803 AIC units and its fitted ν̂ = 2.648 (95% CI 2.43–2.87) implies infinite kurtosis. At the 99% level the Gaussian books an Expected Shortfall of −3.24% where the t books −5.81%, a 79.5% understatement of tail risk.

03
Five models

The NIG wins, and the Laplace nearly ties

All five marginals on the same 6,287 returns. The NIG is the best fit (ΔAIC −1,851) and the only model Kolmogorov–Smirnov doesn't reject. The two-parameter Laplace captures 96% of that gain, most of the improvement over the Gaussian is exponential tails, not thin ones. At FRTB's mandated 97.5% ES the Gaussian sits 28% below the NIG.

Part II · The regression analyses: what you could see coming

A different question from the fitting: does anything observable at day t say how rough the next month will be? A regression of forward 21-day realised volatility on seven trailing factors lifts R² from 0.441 (yesterday's volatility alone) to 0.553, with Newey-West errors to respect the overlapping windows. The VIX does most of the work. The same factors, read in the 21 days before each crisis, tell four very different stories. A second regression asks whether the VIX also predicts the fitted Lévy parameters, and the answer splits cleanly: scale yes, tail shape no.

04
Bayesian

The heavy tail is not estimation noise

Four marginals refitted with full posteriors under NUTS in PyMC, the NIG through a custom Bessel-function log-density. Every model converged cleanly and the posteriors reproduce the MLEs, the headline parameters are stable features of the data, not point-estimate luck.

Validation

The NIG passes every check but one

1,000 replicate return histories per model, scored with posterior predictive p-values across the marginal distribution and one dependence statistic. The failure every model shares, volatility clustering, observed 0.32 against replicate bands on zero, is the limit of any static marginal, and the reason Week 7 exists.

06
Structure

The week has a shape

Calendar and cross-sectional structure. Monday, carrying the weekend gap, has the heaviest tail of the week. The closed market earns a fifth of the variance but takes the jumps. Quarterly NIG skew tracks the VIX (R² 0.64 in logs). And earnings seasons leave index volatility flat to the decimal: index tail risk is macro risk.

07
Backtest

5,787 days out of sample

Each model refitted on a rolling 500-day window, forecasting next-day VaR, judged by Christoffersen's framework. Better marginals genuinely help: the NIG cuts excess 99% violations from ×2.6 to ×1.57. But every model fails the independence test at every level: the violations arrive as bursts, in late 2008 and March 2020.

Were they black swans?

{{ v.window }}

{{ v.verdict }}
I.

The Black Swan is model-relative, and mostly a Gaussian artifact. The same day is four trillion ages of the universe away under the Gaussian and a bad decade under the Student-t.

II.

Not all heavy tails are equal. Exponential-tailed models, excellent by likelihood, misprice the extremes by three to four orders of magnitude. The practical content lives in the power-law region.

III.

What survives the best marginal is timing. Every model finds the clustering of March 2020 a sub-1-in-20,000 event. The honest boundary of iid Lévy modelling is volatility clustering, crossing it requires dynamics.

Postscript: the Lévy prediction itself

the process-level test the title demands

A Lévy process makes one promise the rest of the project never tests: if daily returns are iid NIG, five of them convolve to an exact NIG with δ and μ simply multiplied by five. No new parameters, nothing to tune. The orange curve below is that promise laid over the 1,257 observed 5-day sums; the dashed curve is an NIG fitted to the sums directly. The promise fails exactly where this project keeps finding the boundary: the convolved law is far too thin in the tails, because successive days are not independent. Volatility clustering, in a third guise.
